At the end of every consultation, something is dispatched to the patient. Nobody designed it for the patient.

The After Note is a different answer to that moment. Built from first principles. Shaped entirely around one question: what does this specific person need to understand and do before they leave the building?

Part One
After Notes

The gap that already exists

Every GP system has a dispatch moment. What currently leaves is a byproduct of the record.

Patients leave consultations with incomplete recall of what was said, uncertainty about their medications, and no reliable decision point for when to seek further help. This is not a technology problem. It is a design problem. The infrastructure to solve it already exists in every practice.

For AVT suppliers

The ambient capture moment already generates a structured summary. The After Note is what that summary becomes for the patient, without adding clinical workload.

For GP practices

Safety netting that is specific rather than generic. A documented, defensible record of what the patient was told and when. Fewer follow-up calls about medication instructions.

The design principles

Two rules govern every decision about what goes into an After Note.

Nothing for completeness. Everything for utility.

Every element earns its place by answering one question: would this specific person, in this specific moment, actually use this? The bar is use, not possibility. That question is what makes an After Note different from everything that currently exists in this space.

Nothing that isn't already there.

The After Note contains nothing the clinician did not put into the approved summary. It selects, clarifies, and structures. It does not infer, interpret, or add. The GP's approved note is the boundary. This is both a design principle and a regulatory one.

A worked example

A patient with a respiratory condition, worsening breathing, flying to the Caribbean on Sunday.

They came in scared their holiday was going to be ruined. Here is what the clinical note contained, and what the After Note returned to them.

Clinical note

History

Face to face follow-up for worsening breathing. Pre-existing condition prone to causing breathing issues. Plans to travel to the Caribbean on Sunday. Concerned about enjoying the holiday. Exercises regularly but currently finds any exercise tiring. No red flag symptoms.

Medications

Foster inhaler, carbocisteine.

Examination

Mild wheeze and a few crackles noted.

Diagnosis

Worsening breathing.

Plan

Antibiotics twice daily for seven days. Oral steroids once daily for five days. Take with food. Alongside existing medications.

Safety netting

If not better by Sunday, book Thursday or Friday. If symptoms worsen, return.

After Note

Your breathing and what to do before Sunday

You have been prescribed a short course of antibiotics and steroids to help improve your breathing over the next few days.

What to take

Antibiotics

Take twice a day for 7 days, with breakfast and your evening meal.

Steroids

Take once a day for 5 days, with breakfast. Take with food if possible.

Your usual inhalers and carbocisteine

Continue these as normal.

Before you travel: the aim of this treatment is to improve your breathing before your trip. If you are not improving by Sunday, contact the surgery before travelling.

Get medical help sooner if

  • your breathing gets worse
  • you feel short of breath at rest
  • you develop chest pain
  • you feel significantly more unwell, feverish, or confused
  • you are struggling to speak in full sentences

If symptoms are severe or sudden, seek urgent medical help.

The decisions, explained

Every word in the After Note was a choice. Here is the reasoning behind the key ones.

The title

The clinical note says "worsening breathing." That is a diagnosis. The After Note says "your breathing and what to do before Sunday." That is the reason they came in. The patient did not come in with a respiratory presentation. They came in worried about their trip. The title names what they are actually facing.

The medication cards

The note names antibiotics and steroids but does not tell the patient how to take them in a way they will remember. The cards strip back to what matters: what, when, with what. The existing medications are grouped under "your usual" so nothing feels changed. Focus stays on the two new things they need to remember.

The travel callout

The safety netting in the clinical note is written for the record. The callout is written for the person. It centres the aim of treatment and gives a clear decision point without implying the consultation clinically endorsed travel. The note reflects the situation. It does not make commitments the consultation did not make.

The one link

We could have added NHS 111, the surgery website, a breathing exercises guide, a travel insurance reminder. We added one link. The foreign travel advice page answers the practical question the consultation could not fully address: what do I do if I get ill while away? One link gets clicked. Eight links get ignored.

What was left out and why

These are the things from the clinical note that did not make the After Note. Every omission was a decision.

Mild wheeze and crackles

Examination findings. Written for the clinical record. The patient cannot act on this and it would worry them without purpose.

Diagnosis: worsening breathing

They already know their breathing is worse. Labelling it adds nothing. The After Note addresses the situation, not the classification.

Pre-existing condition detail

They have lived with this condition. They do not need it explained back to them at the end of a consultation.

Exercise currently tiring

They told the GP this. Reflecting it back is noise. The medication addresses it. The After Note focuses on the medication.

Part Two
MyScribe

MyScribe

The After Note starts in the patient's pocket, before the appointment begins.

MyScribe is a free app the patient carries into their appointment. They ask the GP for consent to record. They hit record. They have their appointment. They hit stop. The After Note arrives on their phone before they reach the door.

The patient owns every part of this. Their phone. Their app. Their record. Their After Note.

The consent moment

"Do you mind if I record this, just for my own records?"

This is the patient speaking. The patient is asking the GP. That framing is not incidental. It is the whole point. The patient is not consenting to something being done to them. They are the ones initiating the conversation about consent.

Why this matters

Every existing ambient voice product in the NHS asks the GP to press record. MyScribe puts the record button in the patient's hand. Same technology. Completely different relationship.

Nothing required from the practice

No system integration. No configuration. No training. The patient downloads a free app and brings it to their appointment. The practice receives nothing and does nothing differently.

The recording

The consultation happens. MyScribe listens. That is all.

The patient presses record and puts the phone face down on the desk, or in their pocket. The appointment continues as it always has. The GP does not need to adapt. The patient does not need to manage the technology.

What happens at stop

The patient presses stop as they leave. The recording is processed. The After Note is generated from the conversation and arrives on their phone before they reach Reception. The consultation moment becomes something they carry with them.

The dispatch moment

One consultation. One recording. One After Note. Before the car park.

The After Note does not arrive as a letter days later. It does not require the patient to log into a portal. It arrives in the same pocket as the phone that recorded the consultation, within minutes of the appointment ending.

Behind the work

Neighbourhood CoLab CIC

Four people. Over a century of frontline NHS and local authority experience. One methodology that requires all four lenses at once.

Neighbourhood CoLab CIC was founded by four people who grew up together, went separately into public service, and came back to find the problems in neighbourhood health do not sit cleanly inside any one role or team. The After Note methodology requires clinical safety, psychological understanding, local knowledge, and design thinking simultaneously. No existing team holds all four. That is why the CIC exists.

Nurse

Band 8, 30+ years NHS

Clinical safety and defensibility. Every decision about what goes in and what stays out has to be justifiable. The After Note cannot overrule the clinician, but it has to be accountable in its own right.

Clinical Psychologist

20+ years NHS Mental Health

What a patient can receive depends on what state they're in when they receive it. The After Note is information arriving at a particular emotional moment, and that shapes every word.

Social Worker

20+ years local authority

Knows what happens when the NHS has done its bit and the person is back in their life. Knows which local assets are real. Owns the local layer of every After Note.

Applied Philosopher

Opsimath, NHS Primary Care

Holds the design question: what is this person's experience of this moment, and what do they deserve from it? Responsible for the omission logic and the principle that utility and clarity are the same thing.
